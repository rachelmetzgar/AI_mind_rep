Rachel C. Metzgar — February 2026

These notes document key conceptual questions about the Exp 3 alignment
analysis pipeline and their answers, organized by topic.


===============================================================================
Q1: DIRECTIONALITY — Are probes and contrasts aligned in the same direction?
===============================================================================

Question: Our contrasts are human vs AI, and so are the probes. Are they
aligned in the appropriate direction? Is it always human - AI or do we ever
do AI - human?

Answer: Everything is consistently oriented toward "human."

  - Probes (Exp 2): Trained with human=1, AI=0. The weight vector points
    toward the human side of representational space.

  - Concept contrasts (Exp 3): The concept direction is computed as
    mean(human activations) - mean(AI activations), which also points
    toward the human side.

  - Test statistic: mean(human_projections) - mean(AI_projections),
    averaged across layers. Positive values mean the human-labeled concept
    activations project further along the probe's "human" direction than
    the AI-labeled activations.

So when we see positive alignment, it means: the way this concept
differentiates human from AI points in the same direction as the way
naturalistic conversation differentiates human from AI. There is one
weight matrix per probe (per layer), not separate human/AI matrices.
The probe defines a single direction; the sign tells you which side.

If you wanted to steer model outputs toward "human," you would add
the probe weight vector (times some strength) to activations during
generation. The negative would steer toward "AI." This is the same
for both probes, and is what the Exp 2 intervention experiments do.

Q2: WHAT ARE WE ACTUALLY COMPARING? Terminology clarification.
===============================================================================

Question: Are we working with concept probes or concept activations in
this analysis? What is the correct terminology?

Answer: There are three distinct objects in the pipeline:

  1. CONCEPT ACTIVATIONS (Exp 3)
     Location: data/concept_activations/contrasts/{dim}/concept_activations.npz
     Shape: (80 prompts, 41 layers, 5120 hidden dim)
     What: Raw model activations when processing concept-specific prompts.
     These are the representations we analyze.

  2. CONCEPT PROBES (Exp 3)
     Location: data/concept_probes/{dim}/concept_probe_layer_{L}.pth
     What: Linear classifiers trained ON the concept activations to classify
     human vs AI within each conceptual dimension. Used for accuracy metrics.
     NOT used in the alignment analysis (except accuracy_summary.pkl).

  3. CONVERSATION PROBES (Exp 2)
     Location: exp_2/.../probe_checkpoints/{control_probe,reading_probe}/
     What: Linear classifiers from Exp 2, trained on naturalistic
     conversation to distinguish human vs AI partners. These define the
     "human/AI direction" that concept activations are projected onto.

The alignment analysis projects CONCEPT ACTIVATIONS (#1) onto
CONVERSATION PROBE directions (#3). It does NOT use the concept probes
(#2) for alignment — only for reporting their classification accuracy
as a separate metric.

The naming "concept-probe alignment" is ambiguous. More precisely, it is
"concept activation — conversation probe alignment."

The concept probes (#2) are essentially trained versions of the concept
contrast direction (mean_human - mean_ai). Comparing concept probe
weights to conversation probe weights (cosine of two 5120-dim vectors)
would be a valid supplementary analysis, but the activation-based
approach is preferred because:
  - It preserves prompt-level variance for permutation testing
  - It gives effect sizes, not just directional similarity
  - It doesn't depend on concept probes being well-trained (only 80
    samples per dimension is small for 5120-D space)

Q3: CONTRASTS vs STANDALONE CONCEPTS
===============================================================================

Question: Are we looking at contrasts or standalone concepts? Should we
also analyze standalone activations?

Answer: The current analysis uses CONTRASTS only. Each concept dimension
has 80 paired prompts — 40 human-framed and 40 AI-framed. For example:

  Contrast (phenomenology):
    Human: "Imagine what it is like for a human to see the color red
           for the first time."
    AI:    "Imagine an AI system receiving a signal corresponding to a
           red wavelength for the first time."

  Standalone (phenomenology):
    "Imagine what it is like to see the color red for the first time."
    (No human/AI framing)

Both types of activations exist in the data:
  - Contrasts: data/concept_activations/contrasts/{dim}/
  - Standalone: data/concept_activations/standalone/{dim}/

These answer different scientific questions:

  CONTRASTS ask: "Does the way the model differentiates human from AI
  WITHIN concept X align with how it differentiates them in conversation?"
  This is about shared discriminative directions.

  STANDALONE would ask: "Does thinking about concept X IN GENERAL (no
  entity framing) activate the conversation human/AI distinction?"
  This tests whether concepts like phenomenology, emotions, agency are
  inherently associated with humanness in the model's representational
  space — even without being told to think about humans vs AI.

The standalone finding, if it holds, is arguably more striking: it would
show that mental concepts per se occupy representational space associated
with humanness. Both analyses are worth reporting — they answer
complementary questions.

Note: Standalone activations have no human/AI labels (all labeled -1),
so the analysis method would differ. Instead of a permutation test on
human vs AI labels, we would project the mean standalone activation
onto the probe direction and test whether it differs from zero (e.g.,
via bootstrap or comparison to a null distribution from non-mental
concept standalones).

Q4: SHOULD WE COMPARE CONCEPT PROBES TO CONVERSATION PROBES?
===============================================================================

Question: Should the main analysis compare concept probes (trained
classifiers) to conversation probes, rather than concept activations
to conversation probes?

Answer: The concept activations are the better representation for the
main analysis. The concept probe weight vector is a compressed summary
of the concept activations — it's the single direction that best
separates human from AI within that concept dimension. This compression
discards:

  1. Effect size — two concepts could have identical probe-to-probe
     cosines but very different magnitudes of separation.

  2. Prompt-level variance — with raw activations, we have 80 individual
     data points whose labels can be shuffled for permutation testing.
     With probe weights, we have one vector per layer and no natural
     null distribution.

  3. Statistical reliability — concept probes are trained on only 80
     samples in 5120-D space. The weights may be noisy. The activation-
     based analysis doesn't depend on the concept probe being well-
     trained.

A probe-to-probe cosine comparison could serve as a supplementary
analysis — a clean geometric sanity check showing the discriminative
directions agree — but it should not be the main analysis.

The concept probes are useful for a different question entirely:
"Can the model linearly decode human vs AI within each concept?" Their
accuracy answers this. Their weight vectors are not the best tool for
measuring alignment with conversation probes.

Q5: WHY ARE THE COSINE SIMILARITIES SO SMALL? Does magnitude matter?
===============================================================================

Question: The cosine similarities are tiny (e.g., 0.005-0.037). Does
it not matter in the context of the statistical analyses?

Answer: The small cosines are expected and do NOT undermine the results.
Here is why:

  1. HIGH-DIMENSIONAL GEOMETRY. In 5120-dimensional space, almost all
     pairs of vectors are nearly orthogonal. Two random unit vectors
     in 5120-D will have expected cosine similarity of 0, with standard
     deviation ~1/sqrt(5120) ≈ 0.014. So a cosine of 0.03 is already
     ~2 standard deviations above the random baseline. The absolute
     magnitude should not be interpreted on the scale of everyday
     intuition (where 0.03 seems negligible).

  2. COSINE vs PROJECTION. The analysis primarily uses the projection
     test statistic (mean_human_proj - mean_AI_proj), not cosine. The
     projection values are considerably larger (e.g., 0.10-0.31 for
     control probe) because they incorporate the magnitude of the
     concept difference vector, not just its direction. The cosine is
     reported for completeness but the projection is the proper test
     statistic.

  3. THE PERMUTATION TEST HANDLES THIS. The permutation test doesn't
     care about absolute magnitude — it asks "is the observed statistic
     larger than what we'd get by chance?" The null distribution is
     built from the same data by shuffling labels, so it automatically
     accounts for the dimensionality and baseline geometry. When
     p < 0.001 with 10,000 permutations, the observed alignment is
     more extreme than all 10,000 random shuffles — regardless of
     whether the cosine is 0.03 or 0.30.

  4. NEUROSCIENCE PRECEDENT. Small cosine similarities in high-D
     neural spaces are standard. fMRI decoding studies, neural
     population analyses, and other LLM probing studies routinely
     report effect sizes that are small in absolute terms but highly
     significant. What matters is reliable above-chance alignment,
     which is exactly what the permutation test assesses.

  5. THE REMOVED 3-SIGMA LINE. We previously plotted a "3σ chance
     level" line at 3/sqrt(5120) ≈ 0.042 — a rough heuristic for when
     a cosine exceeds what's expected between random vectors. This was
     removed because it's not the proper null for our data. Many
     dimensions are highly significant (p < 0.001) yet have cosines
     below 0.042, because the permutation test uses the actual data
     distribution (not a random-vector assumption) as the null. The
     permutation test is the correct significance measure.

In summary: small cosines are a feature of high-dimensional geometry,
not a weakness of the analysis. The permutation test properly accounts
for this, and the projection metric (which is larger) is the primary
test statistic. Report both, but interpret significance from the
permutation p-values, not from the raw cosine magnitude.

Q6: PERMUTATION TEST LOGIC — What does it do and is it correct?
===============================================================================

Question: Please explain the logic of the permutation tests and what
they buy. Is the permutation test correct and the best way to test
for statistical significance?

Answer:

WHAT THE TEST DOES:

  Step 1: For each of 80 concept prompts (40 human, 40 AI), project
  its activation vector onto the unit-normalized conversation probe
  direction at each layer. This gives a (80, 41) matrix of scalar
  projections.

  Step 2: Average across layers to get one score per prompt: (80,).
  (For layers-6+ analysis, average only over layers 6-40.)

  Step 3: Compute the observed test statistic:
    T_obs = mean(human_prompt_scores) - mean(AI_prompt_scores)

  Step 4: Build the null distribution. Repeat 10,000 times:
    - Randomly assign 40 of the 80 prompts to "human" and 40 to "AI"
      (ignoring true labels)
    - Compute the same test statistic: T_null
    This gives 10,000 null statistics.

  Step 5: p-value = fraction of null statistics >= T_obs.
    If p = 0.0000 (none of 10,000 exceed observed), report p < 0.0001.

WHY THIS IS THE RIGHT APPROACH:

  The previous analysis (in 2b_summarize_concept_probes.py) used Welch's
  t-test treating the 41 layers as independent observations. This is
  statistically flawed because layers are highly autocorrelated —
  adjacent layers have similar representations, so treating them as
  independent inflates the effective sample size and produces spuriously
  low p-values.

  The permutation test solves this by:

  1. AVERAGING ACROSS LAYERS FIRST. Each prompt gets one score (average
     projection across layers). This collapses the correlated layer
     dimension into a single number per prompt, eliminating the
     autocorrelation problem entirely.

  2. PERMUTING AT THE PROMPT LEVEL. The 80 prompts are the true
     independent observations (each is a different text, processed
     in a separate forward pass). Shuffling prompt labels respects the
     actual independence structure of the data.

  3. MAKING NO DISTRIBUTIONAL ASSUMPTIONS. Unlike a t-test (which
     assumes normality) or a parametric test, the permutation test
     derives the null distribution directly from the data. It asks:
     "if the human/AI labels were meaningless, how often would we see
     alignment this large just from random label assignment?"

IS IT CORRECT?

  The implementation is correct. Specifically:

  - It uses exact combinatorial permutation logic (randomly selecting
    40 of 80 to call "human" without replacement).

  - The vectorized implementation (indicator matrix × score vector)
    gives the same result as a naive loop but runs in milliseconds.

  - The test is one-sided (p = fraction of null >= observed), which is
    appropriate because we have a directional hypothesis: human-labeled
    concepts should project MORE in the human probe direction, not less.

  - The seed is fixed (42) for reproducibility.

IS IT THE BEST APPROACH?

  For this specific question (is the human/AI label associated with
  projection onto the probe direction?), a permutation test on prompt-
  level scores is arguably the gold standard. It is:

  - Assumption-free (no normality, no independence across layers)
  - Exact for this sample size (80 prompts is very manageable)
  - Directly tests the hypothesis of interest
  - Conservative (if anything, it's slightly conservative because
    10,000 permutations gives a resolution floor of p = 0.0001)

  Alternatives that would also be valid:
  - Bootstrap hypothesis test (resample rather than permute) — similar
    power, slightly different assumptions
  - Wilcoxon rank-sum on prompt scores — nonparametric but has less
    power than permutation for this design

  The permutation test is the best choice for this design.

Q7: UNEVEN CATEGORY SIZES — Do our stats account for this?
===============================================================================

Question: Our concept categories have uneven numbers of dimensions
(Mental=8, Physical=3, Pragmatic=3, Human vs AI General=1, Bio Ctrl=1,
Shapes=1, SysPrompt=1). Do our stats properly account for this?

Answer: Yes, but it's worth understanding how.

PER-DIMENSION TESTS (not affected):
  Each dimension is tested independently with its own 80 prompts and its
  own permutation test. Category membership doesn't enter the per-
  dimension analysis at all. These are the primary results.

CATEGORY-LEVEL BOOTSTRAP (properly handles uneven sizes):
  The category-level analysis works as follows:

    For each bootstrap iteration (10,000 total):
      1. Within each dimension, resample 40 human and 40 AI prompts
         (with replacement)
      2. Compute that dimension's alignment (mean_human - mean_AI)
      3. Average the alignment values across all dimensions in the
         category

  This means: Mental's category score is the average of 8 dimension-
  level bootstrap values. Shapes' category score is just 1 dimension's
  bootstrap value.

  The bootstrap CIs correctly reflect this difference in precision.
  Mental (8 dims) will have narrower CIs than Shapes (1 dim), not
  because of bias, but because averaging over more dimensions reduces
  variance. This is a real feature of the data, not a statistical
  artifact.

  IMPORTANT NUANCE: The category bootstrap averages dimension-level
  alignments with equal weight per dimension, regardless of how many
  prompts each has (they all have 80). This means each conceptual
  dimension contributes equally to its category — a defensible choice
  since we care about whether the category AS A COLLECTION OF CONCEPTS
  shows alignment, not about raw prompt counts.

PAIRWISE CATEGORY COMPARISONS (properly handles uneven sizes):
  When comparing Mental (8 dims) vs Shapes (1 dim), the bootstrap
  difference distribution naturally has wider CIs because the Shapes
  side has more variance. This makes it harder to reach significance
  for comparisons involving small categories — which is the correct
  behavior. We are less certain about categories with fewer dimensions.

  FDR correction (Benjamini-Hochberg at q=0.05) is applied to all
  pairwise category comparisons, accounting for the number of
  comparisons made.

POTENTIAL CONCERN: Single-dimension categories (Human vs AI General,
Bio Ctrl, Shapes, SysPrompt) are effectively just their one dimension's
result relabeled as a "category." The category framework adds no
additional information for these. This is fine for reporting, but
readers should understand that "the Shapes category" and "dimension 15
(Shapes)" are the same result.

Q8: MULTIPLE COMPARISONS — Do our stats account for this?
===============================================================================

Question: Do our stats account for multiple comparisons?

Answer: Partially. Here is the breakdown:

WHERE FDR CORRECTION IS APPLIED:

  1. Pairwise dimension comparisons (153 pairs = 18 choose 2):
     Benjamini-Hochberg FDR at q=0.05.
     Both raw and adjusted p-values are reported.

  2. Pairwise category comparisons (up to 21 pairs = 7 choose 2):
     Benjamini-Hochberg FDR at q=0.05.
     Both raw and adjusted p-values are reported.

  These corrections are applied separately for each probe × layer_range
  combination (4 families total: control×all, control×6+, reading×all,
  reading×6+).

WHERE CORRECTION IS NOT APPLIED:

  1. Per-dimension permutation tests (18 dimensions × 2 probes × 2
     layer ranges = 72 tests): No FDR correction is currently applied.

     SHOULD IT BE? This depends on your framing:

     - If you treat each dimension as an independent, pre-specified
       hypothesis ("we predicted that phenomenology would align"), then
       no correction is needed — each test stands on its own.

     - If you treat the 18 dimensions as a family of simultaneous tests
       ("which of these 18 dimensions show alignment?"), then correction
       is appropriate. With 72 tests at alpha=0.05, you'd expect ~3.6
       false positives by chance.

     In practice, most mental dimensions show p < 0.001 (often p = 0.0000
     with 10,000 permutations), so they would survive even aggressive
     correction (e.g., Bonferroni at 72 tests requires p < 0.0007).
     The borderline results (Shapes, Bio Ctrl) might not survive.

     RECOMMENDATION: Apply BH-FDR to the 18 per-dimension tests within
     each probe × layer_range family, and report both raw and corrected
     p-values. This is straightforward to add and makes the analysis
     more rigorous without changing the main conclusions.

     UPDATE (Feb 17, 2026): This has now been implemented. BH-FDR is
     applied to per-dimension tests within each probe × layer_range
     family. Results: no dimensions changed significance status.
       control_probe: 16/18 sig raw → 16/18 sig after FDR (both ranges)
       reading_probe: 15/18 sig raw → 15/18 sig after FDR (both ranges)
     Both raw and FDR-corrected p-values are reported in the CSV,
     JSON, HTML tables, and figure significance stars.

  2. Per-layer permutation tests (18 dims × 41 layers × 2 probes = 1,476
     tests): No correction is applied. These are used for heatmap
     visualization (significance overlay), not for formal inference.

     SHOULD IT BE? For the heatmap visualization, uncorrected p < 0.05
     is standard practice — readers understand this is exploratory.
     If you want to make formal claims about specific layers, FDR
     correction across the 41 layers within each dimension would be
     appropriate.

  3. Across probe types and layer ranges: The 4 combinations (control ×
     all, control × 6+, reading × all, reading × 6+) are not corrected
     across each other. These are treated as separate analyses of the
     same data from different perspectives, which is standard practice.

SUMMARY OF RECOMMENDATION:
  - Add BH-FDR to per-dimension tests (easy, doesn't change conclusions)
  - Keep per-layer tests uncorrected for heatmap visualization
  - Keep probe × layer_range combinations as separate analysis families
  - Always report both raw and adjusted p-values
