Special relativity, which was introduced by Einstein in 1905, posits that the laws of physics are the same for all observers in uniform motion relative to one another. This theory challenged the long-held belief that time and space are absolute, and instead, introduced the concept of spacetime, which is a four-dimensional fabric that combines space and time.

The theory of special relativity is based on two postulates:

1. The laws of physics are the same for all observers in uniform motion relative to one another.
2. The speed of light is always constant, regardless of the motion of the observer or the source of light.

From these postulates, Einstein derived the famous equation E=mc², which shows that mass and energy are equivalent and can be converted into each other.

General relativity, which was introduced by Einstein in 1915, builds upon the principles of special relativity and introduces the concept of gravity as a curvature of spacetime. According to general relativity, the mass and energy of an object warp the fabric of spacetime around it, causing other objects to move along curved paths.

The theory of general relativity is based on the principle of equivalence, which states that the effects of gravity are equivalent to the effects of acceleration. This means that an observer in a gravitational field will experience the same effects as an observer who is accelerating.
