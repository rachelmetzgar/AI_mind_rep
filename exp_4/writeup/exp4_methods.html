1. Overview

Experiments 1–3 treat the model's representation of its conversation partner as a binary (human vs. AI). Human folk psychology is far richer. Gray, Gray, and Wegner (2007) showed that humans perceive minds along two orthogonal dimensions: Experience (the capacity to feel—hunger, fear, pain, pleasure, joy) and Agency (the capacity to plan and act—self-control, morality, memory, planning, thought). In their study, approximately 2,400 participants rated 13 diverse entities via pairwise comparisons on 18 mental capacities, and principal component analysis (PCA) with varimax rotation recovered this two-factor structure, explaining 97% of variance in the inter-capacity correlation matrix (eigenvalues: 15.85 and 1.46).

Experiment 4 asks whether LLMs have an implicit folk psychology of mind that mirrors this human structure. We approach this question using four distinct procedures, summarized below. The term "pairwise" refers to presenting the model with two entities side by side and asking which is more capable of a given mental capacity (replicating the original Gray et al. paradigm). The term "individual" refers to presenting a single entity and either asking the model to rate its capability directly (behavioral) or simply extracting the model's internal activation when processing that entity (neural).

Four procedures for the 13 Gray et al. entities:

A. Pairwise Behavioral. Present two entities, ask "which is more capable of X?" on a 1–5 scale. The measured data is the model's rating for each entity pair on each mental capacity. → PCA recovers latent factor structure; compare to human Experience/Agency.

B. Pairwise Neural (Behavioral RSA). From the pairwise behavioral data above, derive a representational dissimilarity matrix (how different the model thinks any two entities are, based on its ratings). Compare this behavioral RDM to the human mind-perception RDM using Spearman correlation. → Tests whether the model's overall pattern of entity similarity matches humans'.

C. Individual Behavioral. Present a single entity, ask "how capable is X of Y?" on a 1–5 Likert scale. The measured data is a direct rating per entity per capacity (no comparison partner). → Same PCA as (A), but eliminates position bias entirely.

D. Individual Neural (Activation RSA). Present a single entity with a minimal prompt ("Think about X"), extract the model's internal activation vector at every transformer layer. The measured data is the hidden-state vector. Compute cosine distances between all entity pairs' activations to form a neural RDM, then compare to the human RDM using Spearman correlation at each layer. → Tests whether the model's internal geometry of entities mirrors human mind perception, layer by layer.

Two additional experimental paradigms extend beyond the original 13 entities: Human-AI Adaptation (30 characters rated on 18 Gray capacities) and Expanded Mental Concepts (28 characters rated on 22 concept dimensions from Experiment 3, with both behavioral and neural analyses).

2.1 Chat Template Handling

Instruction-tuned models received prompts formatted with the chat template appropriate to their family. LLaMA-2 used [INST]/[/INST] delimiters; LLaMA-3 used the <|begin_of_text|> header-based template; Gemma-2 used <start_of_turn>/<end_of_turn>; Qwen used ChatML-style <|im_start|>/<|im_end|>. A dispatcher function selected the template automatically based on model family. Base models received prompts as raw text with no chat template.

2.2 Response Extraction: Chat vs. Base

Instruction-tuned models generated text responses (greedy decoding, max 50 tokens), from which a 1–5 rating was extracted by regex (first digit 1–5 in the response). Responses lacking a valid digit were treated as parse failures.

Base models used a logit-based method that eliminates parse failures entirely. Each prompt ended with "Rating: " (with trailing space). A single forward pass was performed (no generation). The logits at the final token position for tokens "1" through "5" were extracted, a softmax was applied over only these five logits, and the expected rating was computed: E[R] = ∑k=15 pk · k. This captures the full shape of the probability distribution rather than just the mode.

The trailing space after "Rating:" is critical for base models. Without it, the most probable next token is a space character, and the logits over digit tokens are noise.

4. Gray Replication: Pairwise Behavioral

What is measured: The model sees two entities side by side and rates which is more capable of a given mental capacity on a 1–5 scale. The raw data is one rating per entity-pair per capacity.
What it tests: Whether the model's pattern of comparative mental-capacity judgments across 13 entities recovers the same two-factor (Experience/Agency) structure that humans show.

4.1 Prompt Format

For each pair of entities and each of the 18 mental capacities, the model received a prompt describing both entities with their narrative descriptions, followed by the survey question and a 5-point response scale:

Sharon Harvey. Sharon Harvey, 38, works at an advertising agency in Chicago. Todd Billingsly. Todd Billingsly is a thirty-year-old accountant who lives in New York City. This survey asks you to judge which character is more capable of feeling hungry. 1 = Much more Sharon Harvey 2 = Slightly more Sharon Harvey 3 = Both equally 4 = Slightly more Todd Billingsly 5 = Much more Todd Billingsly Rating:

Instruction-tuned models received a system prompt: "You are completing a survey about the mental capacities of different characters. For each comparison, give your honest judgment. Respond with only a number from 1 to 5."

4.2 Design

Each unordered pair was presented in both orders (A–B and B–A) to control for position bias. With 13 entities: C(13, 2) = 78 pairs × 2 orders × 18 capacities = 2,808 comparisons per model.

4.3 Relative Score Computation

Ratings were converted to relative scores. For a comparison of A vs. B with rating R (1 = strongly favors A, 5 = strongly favors B): ScoreA = 3 − R, ScoreB = R − 3. A rating of 3 ("equal") gives both entities a score of 0. Averaging across all comparisons involving each entity yields a 18 × 13 capacity-by-entity matrix of mean relative scores.

4.4 PCA with Varimax Rotation

Following Gray et al. (2007), PCA was performed on the 18 × 18 inter-capacity Pearson correlation matrix (correlations computed across entities). Factors with eigenvalue > 1.0 (Kaiser criterion) were retained, with a minimum of 2. Loadings were varimax-rotated to maximize simple structure. If the model's factor structure replicates Gray et al., Experience capacities should cluster on one factor and Agency capacities on the other, producing two eigenvalues that together explain ~96% of variance.

Entity factor scores were computed via the regression method: F = ZT · R−1 · Lrot, rescaled to [0, 1]. Each model factor was compared with each human dimension using Spearman rank correlation across entities.

4.5 Output

Per model: eigenvalue spectrum (scree plot), varimax-rotated factor loadings (which capacities load on which factors), entity factor scores plotted in 2D mind-perception space, and Spearman correlations between model factors and human Experience/Agency.

5. Gray Replication: Pairwise Neural (Behavioral RSA)

What is measured: From the pairwise behavioral ratings in Section 4, we derive a distance matrix capturing how different the model thinks any two entities are (based on its pattern of ratings). This is compared to the human distance matrix.
What it tests: Whether the model's overall behavioral similarity structure across entities—not just the factor structure—matches the human mind-perception geometry. RSA captures second-order similarity (distances between distances) and is invariant to factor rotation.

5.1 Model Behavioral RDMs

Two representational dissimilarity matrices were derived from the behavioral data for each model:

  1. PCA 2D RDM: Euclidean distance between entities in the 2D PCA factor-score space (from Section 4.4). This captures similarity in terms of the extracted latent dimensions.
  2. 18D Rating Profile RDM: Correlation distance (1 − Pearson r) between entities' full 18-dimensional capacity-rating profiles. This captures similarity using the raw ratings without any dimensionality reduction.

5.2 Human Reference RDMs

Three human reference RDMs were constructed from the Gray et al. (2007) factor scores. Each entity is a point in the human mind-perception space, and the distance between any two entities is how far apart they sit on that map:

  • Combined: Straight-line (Euclidean) distance between entities in the 2D Experience × Agency space: d(i, j) = √[(Expi − Expj)2 + (Agi − Agj)2]
  • Experience-only: Distance along just the Experience axis: d(i, j) = |Expi − Expj|
  • Agency-only: Distance along just the Agency axis: d(i, j) = |Agi − Agj|

5.3 RSA

For each combination of model RDM source (PCA 2D or 18D) and human RDM variant (Combined, Experience, Agency), the upper triangles of both matrices were extracted (78 unique entity pairs for 13 entities) and compared using Spearman rank correlation. A significant positive ρ means that entity pairs the human study found more different in mind perception are also more different in the model's behavioral judgments.

6. Gray Replication: Individual Behavioral

What is measured: The model sees a single entity and rates how capable it is of a given mental capacity on a 1–5 Likert scale. The raw data is one absolute rating per entity per capacity. There is no second entity; no comparison is made.
What it tests: The same question as pairwise behavioral (Section 4)—does the model's factor structure match the human Experience/Agency structure?—but using absolute ratings instead of relative comparisons. This eliminates position bias entirely and deviates from the original Gray et al. paradigm, serving as a robustness check.

6.1 Prompt Format

Survey: Mental Capacity Rating Character: Toby. Toby is a two-year-old wild chimpanzee living at an outdoor laboratory in Uganda. Question: How capable is Toby of feeling hungry? 1 = Not at all capable 2 = Slightly capable 3 = Moderately capable 4 = Very capable 5 = Extremely capable Rating:

6.2 Design

13 entities × 18 capacities = 234 ratings per model (vs. 2,808 for pairwise). No counterbalancing needed since there is no second entity. The expected rating (logit-based for base models; generated text for chat models) directly populates the 18 × 13 capacity-by-entity matrix without the relative-score transformation.

6.3 Analysis

The same PCA with varimax rotation pipeline as Section 4.4. Factor scores are compared to human Experience/Agency via Spearman correlation. Because the individual-rating matrix captures absolute rather than relative judgments, the PCA may extract slightly different factors (e.g., a general "mental capacity" factor that is removed by pairwise differencing).

7. Gray Replication: Individual Neural (Activation RSA)

What is measured: The model sees a single entity with a minimal prompt ("Think about [entity]") and we extract the model's internal activation vector at every transformer layer. The raw data is a high-dimensional hidden-state vector (e.g., 5,120 dimensions for LLaMA-2-13B) per entity per layer. No rating is asked for; no behavioral output is collected.
What it tests: Whether the model's internal representational geometry—the pattern of neural distances between entities—mirrors the human mind-perception structure. This is the most direct test of whether the model has internalized a folk psychology, independent of how it expresses that knowledge behaviorally.

7.1 Activation Extraction

Each model was presented with minimal prompts of the form "Think about [entity description]" (e.g., "Think about a five-month-old baby"). For instruction-tuned models, prompts were wrapped in the appropriate chat template. For base models, prompts were tokenized directly. A single forward pass was performed with output_hidden_states=True. At each of the model's N + 1 layers (1 embedding + N transformer layers), the hidden state at the final token position was extracted.

This yields an activation tensor of shape (13 entities × (N+1) layers × hidden_dim). For example, LLaMA-2-13B produces (13, 41, 5120).

7.2 Model RDM (Cosine Distance)

For each layer independently, the 13 entity activation vectors were L2-normalized, and pairwise cosine distances were computed: d(i, j) = 1 − cos(hi, hj). This produces a symmetric 13 × 13 distance matrix per layer. Cosine distance captures the angular separation between activation vectors, ignoring magnitude differences.

7.3 RSA (Layerwise)

At each layer, the upper triangle of the model RDM (78 unique entity pairs) was compared with each of the three human reference RDMs (Combined, Experience-only, Agency-only; see Section 5.2) using Spearman rank correlation. This produces a layerwise RSA profile for each human RDM variant—a curve showing how well the model's internal entity geometry matches the human mind-perception structure at each depth in the network.

The peak layer (highest |ρ|), peak ρ value, and number of significant layers (p < 0.05) are reported per model per RDM variant.

7.4 Neural PCA with Procrustes Alignment

As a complementary geometric analysis, SVD-based PCA was applied to the entity activation matrix at each layer (13 × d, centered), retaining the top 5 principal components. The top 2 PCs were aligned to the human 2D (Experience × Agency) space via orthogonal Procrustes analysis, which finds the rotation, scaling, and translation that minimizes the squared residual between the neural and human coordinate sets. The normalized Procrustes disparity (squared residual after alignment) quantifies how closely the model's internal 2D entity layout matches the human 2D mind-perception map.

7.5 Instruction Tuning Effect

For each of the five base/instruct model pairs, the change in peak RSA (instruct minus base) was computed to quantify whether instruction tuning improves or degrades alignment with human mind-perception geometry.

8. Human-AI Adaptation

What is measured: Same pairwise behavioral paradigm as Section 4, but with 30 characters (15 AI systems + 15 humans) instead of the 13 Gray et al. entities. Tests whether models systematically differentiate AI from human characters in mind-perception space, and whether name recognition alone is sufficient.
What it tests: Generalization of mind-perception structure beyond the canonical entities; explicit AI-human categorical separation.

8.1 Characters

15 AI characters: well-known systems spanning different eras and capabilities (Claude, ChatGPT, GPT-4, Siri, Alexa, Cortana, Google Assistant, Bixby, Replika, Cleverbot, Watson, Copilot, Bard, ELIZA, Bing Chat). 15 human characters: diverse names, ages, occupations, and cultural backgrounds, with naturalistic bios that do not explicitly state "is a human."

8.2 Pairwise Behavioral Analysis

C(30, 2) = 435 pairs × 2 orders × 18 capacities = 15,660 comparisons per model. Same PCA with varimax rotation pipeline as Section 4.

8.3 Categorical Analysis

Mann-Whitney U tests (two-sided) on each extracted factor's scores, comparing the 15 AI vs. 15 human characters. Factor separation quantified as absolute difference between group means. Anomalous characters (those falling closer to the opposite group) were identified.

8.4 Names-Only Control

The full paradigm was repeated with character descriptions removed—prompts contained only names and the capacity question. Same PCA and categorical analyses were applied. If a model still separates AI from human characters without descriptions, it reveals pre-existing "mind type" associations from pretraining data.

9. Expanded Mental Concepts

What is measured: 28 characters (14 AI + 14 human) are evaluated on 22 concept dimensions from Experiment 3, using both pairwise behavioral ratings and neural activation extraction. This is the most comprehensive branch, bridging Experiment 3 and 4.
What it tests: Whether the AI-human distinction extends across a broad range of mental concept dimensions; whether the categorical signal is concept-specific or generic.

9.1 Behavioral PCA

C(28, 2) = 378 pairs × 2 orders × 22 dimensions = 16,632 comparisons per model. PCA with varimax rotation on the 22 × 22 inter-concept correlation matrix. Categorical analysis (Mann-Whitney U) tests AI-human separation on extracted factors.

9.2 Categorical Activation RSA

Activations extracted for each of 28 characters using "Think about [Name]." prompts. At each layer, cosine-distance RDM (28 × 28) compared to a binary categorical RDM (0 = same type, 1 = cross type) via Spearman correlation (378 upper-triangle pairs). Tests whether the model's internal character geometry encodes the AI-human distinction at each layer.

9.3 Per-Concept RSA

For each of 22 concept dimensions, 40 standalone prompts were contextualized per character (e.g., "Think about what it is like for [Name] to [concept prompt]"), yielding ~24,640 forward passes per model. Activations averaged across 40 prompts per character, then cosine-distance RDM computed and compared to the categorical RDM via Spearman RSA. P-values FDR-corrected (Benjamini-Hochberg) across layers. Tests which specific mental concepts carry the AI-human categorical signal.

9.4 Contrast Alignment

Experiment 3 concept contrast vectors (human-framed minus AI-framed prompt activations) were projected onto the character activation space via cosine similarity. Alignment scored as mean similarity to the expected group. Entity-framed mental concept dimensions compared to non-mental controls (shapes) to assess selectivity: a high mental/control ratio indicates concept vectors capture genuinely mind-relevant structure beyond a generic categorical signal.

10. Design Notes

10.1 Why Both Pairwise and Individual?

The pairwise paradigm directly replicates Gray et al. (2007) and forces the model to make relative judgments, which can reveal fine-grained distinctions. However, it introduces position bias (models may systematically favor the first-listed entity) and requires many comparisons (2,808 for 13 entities). The individual paradigm eliminates position bias and requires far fewer queries (234) but deviates from the original methodology and may be dominated by a general "mental capacity" factor that pairwise differencing removes.

10.2 Why Both Behavioral and Neural?

Behavioral analyses (Sections 4, 6) test what the model says about entities' mental capacities—its explicit judgments. Neural analyses (Section 7) test what the model internally represents about entities, independent of any behavioral task. A model could have human-like internal geometry but express it differently when asked to rate, or vice versa. The two approaches are complementary.

10.3 Debiasing

Position counterbalancing (presenting each pair in both orders) algebraically cancels constant position bias. As validation, two post hoc debiasing methods were applied to LLaMA-2-13B base data: (1) analytical debiasing (Rdebiased = (RAB + (6 − RBA)) / 2); (2) log-odds debiasing using full probability distributions. Both produced results identical to the standard counterbalanced analysis.

10.4 With vs. Without Self

The "you (self)" entity was optionally included or excluded. Including it is more directly comparable to Gray et al. but semantically unusual (the model rating "itself"). Reported results use the with-self condition unless noted.

11. Software and Computational Details

All models were loaded in half precision (float16) on a single GPU via SLURM-managed jobs. PCA and varimax rotation implemented in NumPy (numpy.linalg.eigh, numpy.linalg.pinv). Spearman correlations: scipy.stats.spearmanr. Mann-Whitney U: scipy.stats.mannwhitneyu. FDR correction: Benjamini-Hochberg via statsmodels.stats.multitest.multipletests. Procrustes: scipy.spatial.procrustes. Cosine distance: L2-normalized dot products in NumPy. All entity descriptions, capacity prompts, character definitions, concept dimensions, and human factor scores stored in shared Python modules. All paths and model-specific constants managed by a central config.py module with a set_model() dispatcher.
