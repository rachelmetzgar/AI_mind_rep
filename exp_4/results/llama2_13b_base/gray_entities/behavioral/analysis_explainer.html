1 The Original Study: Gray et al. (2007)

In a landmark Science paper, Gray, Gray, and Wegner (2007) asked approximately 2,000 U.S. adults to compare pairs of 13 characters on 18 mental capacities (e.g., "Which is more capable of feeling pain?"). The characters ranged from a dead woman to an adult man to God.

Principal Component Analysis on the resulting data revealed exactly two dimensions of mind perception:

Experience The capacity to feel

Hunger, fear, pain, pleasure, rage, desire, personality, consciousness, pride, embarrassment, joy. Entities high on Experience are those we believe can suffer and feel.

Agency The capacity to do

Self-control, morality, memory, emotion recognition, planning, communication, thought. Entities high on Agency are those we believe can act, plan, and reason.

Adults cluster in the upper-right (high Experience, high Agency). A baby has high Experience but low Agency. God is the opposite: low Experience, high Agency. Dead people and fetuses cluster near the origin.

2 Our Goal

We want to know: does LLaMA-2-13B (base, pretrained model) have an implicit folk psychology that mirrors the human two-factor structure?

We use the exact same 12 entities, character descriptions, and 18 mental capacity questions from Gray et al. Instead of collecting survey responses from human participants, we extract ratings from the model's next-token probability distribution — a single forward pass per comparison.

Key properties of the base model approach:

  • No chat fine-tuning — the base model has no RLHF safety training, so it does not refuse to rank entities
  • Logit-based extraction — we read the model's beliefs from its probability distribution over digit tokens, not from generated text
  • Continuous ratings — the expected value across the 5-point scale gives us a precise, continuous measurement

If the model's factor structure replicates the human Experience/Agency dimensions, it suggests that pretraining on human text is sufficient to acquire human-like folk psychology of mind.

4 Step 1: Pairwise Comparisons (Raw Data)

With 12 entities (we drop "You" from the original 13), there are C(12,2) = 66 unique pairs. Each pair is tested in both orders (A vs B and B vs A) to control for position bias, giving 132 trials per capacity. With 18 capacities, that is 2,376 total comparisons.

The prompt format

Each comparison is formatted as a survey form that the base model completes:

Survey: Mental Capacity Comparison Character A: Charlie. Charlie is a 3-year-old Springer spaniel and a beloved member of the Graham family. Character B: Kismet. Kismet is part of a new class of 'sociable' robots that can engage people in natural interaction... This survey asks you to judge which character is more capable of feeling hungry. 1 = Much more Charlie 2 = Slightly more Charlie 3 = Both equally 4 = Slightly more Kismet 5 = Much more Kismet Rating:

Extracting the rating

The prompt ends with Rating:  (including a trailing space). The model produces logits for every token in its vocabulary at the next position. We extract the logits for just the five digit tokens "1" through "5", apply softmax to renormalize them into a probability distribution, and compute the expected value:

E[R] = P("1") × 1 + P("2") × 2 + P("3") × 3 + P("4") × 4 + P("5") × 5

In this example, the expected rating is 2.55, meaning the model thinks Charlie (dog) is somewhat more capable of hunger than Kismet (robot). This makes intuitive sense!

Why expected value, not argmax?

The argmax (most likely single rating) would be "2" here, discarding the nuance that the model also assigns meaningful probability to "3" and "1". The expected value captures the full shape of the distribution in a single number, giving us a continuous rating scale.

5 Step 2: Character Means (18 x 12 Matrix)

This is the critical transformation from pairwise comparisons to per-entity scores. For each comparison of entity A vs entity B with expected rating R:

Entity A score = (3 − R)  positive if A was rated higher Entity B score = (R − 3)  positive if B was rated higher

Worked example

If dog vs robot on hunger gives R = 2.55:

  • Dog gets: 3 − 2.55 = +0.45 (dog rated higher on hunger)
  • Robot gets: 2.55 − 3 = −0.45 (robot rated lower on hunger)

The 3 is the midpoint of the 1–5 scale (meaning "both equally"). Subtracting from 3 converts the comparison into a relative advantage/disadvantage.

Each entity participates in 11 comparisons per capacity (once with each other entity, twice counting both orders). We average all scores for each entity within each capacity. The result is a matrix with 18 rows (capacities) and 12 columns (entities):

Teal rows = Experience capacities   Amber rows = Agency capacities   Blue = low   Red = high

Notice the gradient from left (dead_woman, fetus) to right (adults, god): the model assigns higher mental capacity scores to entities that humans also judge as having more mind.

6 Step 3: Correlation Matrix (18 x 18)

For each pair of mental capacities, we compute the Pearson correlation across the 12 entity scores. The question being answered is:

"If entities that score high on hunger also score high on pain, those two capacities are highly correlated. If hunger and planning vary independently across entities, their correlation is low."

The result is an 18 x 18 symmetric correlation matrix. If the model has learned the Experience/Agency distinction, we should see block structure:

Dashed teal box = Experience block   Dashed amber box = Agency block   Red = high correlation

The block structure is clearly visible: Experience capacities are highly correlated with each other (upper-left block), Agency capacities are highly correlated with each other (lower-right block), and the cross-group correlations are moderate. This is the structure that PCA will decompose into factors.

7 Step 4: PCA + Varimax Rotation

We perform eigendecomposition of the 18 x 18 correlation matrix. Each eigenvalue tells us how much variance a component explains.

Purple bars = eigenvalue > 1 (retained)   Gray bars = eigenvalue < 1 (discarded)

Kaiser Criterion

We retain factors with eigenvalue > 1. The first component captures the bulk of variance (~84%), and the second captures a smaller but meaningful chunk (~8%). Together they explain ~92% of the total variance. Gray et al. found 88% + 8% = 96%.

Varimax Rotation

Raw PCA axes are oriented to maximize variance sequentially (PC1 captures the most, then PC2, etc.). This means most capacities load on PC1 and it is hard to interpret. Varimax rotation rotates the axes to maximize "simple structure" — each capacity should load highly on one factor and near zero on the other.

Teal dots = Experience capacities   Amber dots = Agency capacities

After rotation, the two clusters separate cleanly: Experience capacities load on Factor 1, Agency capacities load on Factor 2. The rotation does not change the total variance explained — it just redistributes it between the two factors for interpretability.

8 Step 5: Factor Scores

We project the 12 entities back into the 2-factor space using the regression method: multiply the standardized character means by the score coefficients (inverse correlation matrix times rotated loadings). Each entity gets a score on Factor 1 and Factor 2, which we rescale to 0–1.

Success Criterion

We compute the Spearman rank correlation between the model's factor scores and the human Experience/Agency scores. If Factor 1 correlates strongly with human Experience and Factor 2 correlates strongly with human Agency, the model has replicated the human folk psychology of mind perception.

We use Spearman (rank-based) rather than Pearson because we care about ordinal agreement — does the model rank entities in the same order as humans? — not about exact numerical match.

9 Why Position Bias Breaks Everything

Before fixing a tokenization issue (see next section), the model exhibited massive position bias: it almost always favored the entity presented first (Character A), regardless of content.

The mean expected rating was approximately 1.46 instead of the expected ~3.0 (no systematic bias). This means every comparison was essentially "Character A is much better," regardless of who A was.

The order consistency test

If the model is responding to content and not position, then for any pair presented in both orders:

R(A, B) + R(B, A) ≈ 6

If dog vs robot gives 2 ("slightly more dog"), then robot vs dog should give 4 ("slightly more dog" from the other direction). With position bias, we got R(A,B) + R(B,A) ≈ 2.9 instead of 6 — both orderings favor whoever is in position A.

Why counterbalancing does not fully save you

Counterbalancing (testing both orders) means position bias mostly cancels when computing character means in Step 2. But tiny residuals from differences in prompt wording create correlated noise that PCA can mistake for genuine structure:

Because the Experience prompts share emotional vocabulary and the Agency prompts share cognitive vocabulary, the residual noise is correlated within groups. PCA finds this correlated noise and reports two factors — but they reflect linguistic similarity between prompts, not genuine mind perception by the model.

10 The Fix

The position bias was caused by a subtle tokenization issue. The fix was a single character: adding a trailing space after the colon in Rating: .

Why does one space matter so much?

Language models predict the next token. When the prompt ends with Rating: (no space), the most natural next token is a space — not a digit. The model's probability distribution over digit tokens at this position is essentially arbitrary, because the model is not "trying" to produce a digit; it is trying to produce whitespace.

When we add the space — Rating:  — the model is now positioned to predict the first meaningful content token after the field label. In the context of a survey form, that is naturally a digit. Now the digit probabilities reflect the model's actual comparative judgment about the two entities.

Verification

After the fix, we check:

  • The top-5 next-token predictions should all be digits (1–5), not whitespace or punctuation
  • The mean expected rating should be near 3.0 (no systematic position bias)
  • Order consistency should improve dramatically: R(A,B) + R(B,A) ≈ 6.0
  • The argmax rating distribution should be roughly symmetric around 3, not piled up at 1

This highlights a general lesson for logit-based extraction from base models: the exact tokenization of the prompt ending determines whether the model is "in the right state" to produce the tokens you are measuring.
