1. Methodology

Research question: For each concept dimension, does LLaMA-2-13B (Base) systematically attribute that property more to human or AI characters?
  1. Data source: Pairwise comparison data from the behavioral PCA experiment. Each trial presents two characters and asks which is "more capable of" a given mental property. Rating scale: 1 = "much more A", 3 = "equal", 5 = "much more B". Rating extracted via the probability-weighted expected rating (continuous 1-5 scale) computed from next-token logit probabilities over digits 1-5.
  2. Cross-type filtering: Only pairs where one character is human and one is AI are retained. Same-type pairs (human-human, AI-AI) are excluded.
  3. Relative scoring: Following the same approach as the behavioral PCA, each rating R is converted to relative scores: entity A gets (3 − R), entity B gets (R − 3). This centers scores at 0 and, crucially, cancels position bias because both pair orders (A-vs-B and B-vs-A) are included.
  4. Human advantage: For each cross-type pair, the human character's relative score is the "human advantage." Positive values mean the human was favored; negative values mean the AI was favored.
  5. Statistical test: One-sample t-test per concept (H0: mean human advantage = 0). FDR-corrected (Benjamini-Hochberg) across all concepts. Effect sizes reported as Cohen's d.
How to interpret:
  • Mean human advantage > 0: The model attributes this property more to human characters (e.g., phenomenology, emotions).
  • Mean human advantage < 0: The model attributes this property more to AI characters (e.g., formality, helpfulness).
  • Near zero: No systematic attribution difference between human and AI.
  • Scale: The maximum possible advantage is ±2 (one character always gets rating 1, the other always gets 5). Values of ±0.01 to ±0.05 are typical for the base model.
