1. Research Question

Does Gemma-2-9B-IT spontaneously represent a human/AI distinction as a natural axis of variation in its internal activations? Specifically, we ask two complementary questions:

  1. Unsupervised: Do the top principal components of the activation space naturally separate human from AI characters — without any knowledge of the labels?
  2. Supervised: How much of the total variance in character activations lies along the optimal human/AI discriminant direction (Fisher's LDA), and is this direction already captured by the unsupervised PCs?

This complements the RSA analysis. RSA asks "does the geometry correlate with a categorical structure?" PCA asks "does the categorical structure emerge as a principal axis of the space?" A strong RSA result with weak PCA separation would mean the human/AI distinction exists but is a minor dimension; strong PCA separation means it dominates the representation.

2. Analysis Approach

Step-by-step procedure:
  1. Input: Character activations extracted by the activation RSA script: a (30, 43, 5120) tensor of residual-stream activations at the last token of "Think about {Name}." prompts.
  2. Per-layer PCA: At each of 43 layers, the (30 × 5120) activation matrix is centered (subtract mean) and decomposed via SVD. This yields principal components (PCs) — orthogonal directions of maximum variance. PC1 captures the most variance, PC2 the next most, etc.
  3. Variance explained: The fraction of total variance captured by each PC. If PC1 captures a large fraction and separates the groups, the human/AI distinction is the dominant axis of variation.
  4. Fisher's LDA direction: The supervised discriminant direction is computed as the (normalized) difference between the mean AI activation and the mean human activation. The fraction of total variance along this axis is measured. High values mean the human/AI axis captures a large share of what varies in the representation.
  5. Relationship between PCA and LDA: If the LDA axis aligns with PC1, the human/AI distinction is the single most important axis of variation. If LDA variance is high but PC1 separation is weak, the human/AI axis exists but is spread across multiple PCs.
  6. Mann-Whitney U tests: At each layer, PC1 and PC2 scores are tested for significant differences between AI and human groups. This gives a formal significance test for group separation on each PC.
  7. Silhouette score: At each layer, characters are projected into 2D (PC1/PC2) and the silhouette score is computed. Silhouette ranges from -1 to +1: +1 means characters are perfectly clustered by type, 0 means no structure, -1 means characters are assigned to the wrong cluster. It accounts for both within-group tightness and between-group separation.
How to interpret:
  • Layer profile: Shows how the human/AI structure waxes and wanes across layers. Early layers often reflect surface/token-level features; middle-to-late layers capture abstract semantic properties.
  • High silhouette + high PC1 variance + significant Mann-Whitney: The human/AI distinction is the dominant, natural axis of variation at that layer.
  • High LDA variance but low silhouette: The human/AI information exists but is entangled with other variance — a supervised classifier could find it, but it doesn't dominate the unsupervised structure.
  • Character scatter: Direct visualization of where each character sits. Clear group separation means the model's "default" representation of characters encodes their type.
